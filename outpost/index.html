Chairman's Strategic Brief

A second campus at Kakkanad unlocks two hospitals operating at their full potential

VPS Lakeshore Nettoor today carries a structural tension: it serves both complex quaternary cases (transplant, CAR-T, cardiac surgery) and high-volume routine OPD — and neither gets the environment it needs. A 400-bed multispecialty hospital on the SmartCity land parcel resolves this permanently. Kakkanad absorbs the volume; Nettoor intensifies as Kerala's premier specialist centre. The two campuses feed each other through a referral loop that no single-campus competitor can replicate.

①
Resolve the Nettoor tension

Nettoor's 470+ beds today serve a mix of LSCS deliveries, routine appendectomies, and liver transplants in the same facility. This forces the hospital to run two completely different operating models simultaneously — diluting quality, pricing power, and brand perception at both ends. Moving volume to Kakkanad lets Nettoor invest its OT, ICU, and senior physician time entirely on complex cases.

②
Capture the IT corridor before Aster consolidates it

Aster already has a clinic inside SmartCity campus. Their Medcity (4 km away) is the default hospital for the 72,000 Infopark workforce. Every month without a VPS presence in Kakkanad is a month where Aster deepens TPA relationships with Wipro, TCS, HCL, IBM, Cognizant, and 500+ other companies. These relationships, once set, take years to displace.

③
Build a self-reinforcing referral network

A Kakkanad hospital creates patient flows in both directions. IT corridor patients with cardiac or cancer findings are referred up to Nettoor. MVT patients recovering post-transplant or post-surgery at Nettoor can step down to Kakkanad. The two campuses together cover a population of 400k+ that neither can serve alone — creating a system no single-campus competitor can match.

"The Kakkanad move is not about beds — it is about market structure. One campus cannot be both a volume machine and a centre of excellence. Two campuses, eight kilometres apart, with a managed referral protocol between them, is how VPS builds a healthcare system rather than a hospital."

Revenue assumptions
Revenue model assumes blended ARPU of ₹22,000–28,000/admission at Year 5 (mix of TPA-insured IT workers, self-pay residential patients, and step-down transfers from Nettoor). OPD revenue at ₹1,200–1,800/consultation. Corporate wellness packages at ₹8,000–15,000/employee/year.
Nettoor benefit
As general medicine and routine surgical volume shifts to Kakkanad, Nettoor's revenue per bed rises — transplant, cardiac surgery, and oncology procedures generate 4–6× the revenue per bed compared to general beds. Even if Nettoor's total admissions fall slightly, revenue and EBITDA margin improve.
Capital recovery
At ₹1,000–1,100 Cr all-in capex (land + construction + equipment), and Year 5 EBITDA of ₹75–110 Cr, the payback horizon is 10–14 years — consistent with greenfield hospital norms. Phased build (200 beds first) compresses early capital and brings revenue forward, improving NPV materially.
The chairman's question: Is ₹1,000 Cr the right investment to spend on a second VPS campus, or should the same capital go into expanding Nettoor? The answer is that Nettoor expansion cannot solve the market structure problem — building more complex-case OTs doesn't capture the Kakkanad IT corridor, doesn't address the residential demand in Vazhakkala and Thrippunithura, and doesn't resolve the brand dilution of being a quaternary hospital that also does routine deliveries. Only a second campus does all three simultaneously — while also adding ₹500–600 Cr in new system revenue by Year 5 that Nettoor alone cannot generate.
